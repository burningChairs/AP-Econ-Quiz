  • What does GDP measure in a country’s economy?
  • The total value of all financial assets
  • The total income earned by citizens abroad
  • The total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a given period
    • Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is the broadest quantitative measure of a nation’s total economic activity. It represents the monetary value of all final goods and services produced within a country’s borders over a specific time period.
  • The total number of workers in the labor force
  • Which of the following would be counted in a country’s GDP?
  • The purchase of a used car
  • A homemaker’s unpaid domestic work
  • The construction of a new factory
    • GDP includes the value of new production within the country. A new factory contributes to investment spending, which is one of the components of GDP. Used goods and financial asset purchases are not counted.
  • An individual’s purchase of foreign stocks
  • What does the unemployment rate represent?
  • The number of people who are not working
  • The percentage of the adult population that is not working
  • The percentage of people in the labor force who are unemployed and actively seeking work
    • The unemployment rate is calculated as the number of unemployed individuals actively looking for a job divided by the total labor force. It does not include people who are not seeking work (e.g., retirees, students).
  • The number of retirees and students not in the labor force
  • If the central bank wants to reduce inflation, which of the following is it most likely to do?
  • Lower the reserve requirement
  • Buy government bonds
  • Lower interest rates
  • Raise interest rates
    • Raising interest rates is a contractionary monetary policy tool. It makes borrowing more expensive, reduces consumer spending and investment, and helps slow down inflationary pressures in the economy.
  • Which of the following is an example of fiscal policy?
  • The central bank increases the money supply
  • The government cuts taxes to stimulate demand
    • Fiscal policy refers to government decisions on taxation and spending. Cutting taxes increases households’ disposable income, encouraging spending and potentially boosting aggregate demand.
  • Commercial banks lower mortgage rates
  • The stock market adjusts to inflation expectations
